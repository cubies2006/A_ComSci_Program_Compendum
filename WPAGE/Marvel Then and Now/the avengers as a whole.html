    As A Whole

         Kirby stayed with the comic for its first year, relinquishing art chores to Don Heck with the September, 1964 issue, but did occasional page layouts during its second year. Lee stayed on a while longer. In the years following, the series has been written by Roy Thomas, Steve Englehart, Kurt Busiek, and many other top comics writers; and drawn by such stellar artists as George Pérez, John Buscema and Neal Adams.

         The Hulk — not exactly a team player — quit in issue #2. In the fourth issue, Captain America, freshly revived from a state of suspended animation he'd entered during the closing days of World War II, joined. Seven issues after that, Spider-Man became the first superhero to be offered membership, and turn it down. By this time, the group was firmly ensconced in its first headquarters, a Manhattan mansion owned by Tony Stark (Iron Man's secret identity) and staffed by Edwin Jarvis, the butler who was to remain a supporting character for many years to come.

         The 16th issue (May, 1965) contained the first of many total upheavals in membership. All of the charter members resigned, leaving only Captain America. Their places were taken by three reformed super-villains, seeking a chance to atone for their misdeeds. Hawkeye, a super-bowman reminiscent of DC's Green Arrow, had formerly plagued Iron Man. Quicksilver and The Scarlet Witch, brother and sister, were fleeing the Brotherhood of Evil Mutants, a group of X-Men bad guys. All three were to remain with The Avengers for years to come. (The reason for jettisoning the most popular characters probably had to do with distribution limitations — Marvel was limited in how many comics per month it could publish, and it was apparently decided that keeping minor characters in the public eye was a better use of one of them, than featuring those that appeared regularly elsewhere.)

         All of the founders (except, of course, The Hulk) eventually came back (after Marvel overcame its distribution problem, in 1968), and most have drifted in and out ever since. Others who have been Avengers members over the years include (but are by no means limited to) The Beast, The Vision, Wonder Man, Hercules, Tigra, Starfox, The Sub-Mariner, She-Hulk, Patsy Walker, The Black Knight … The list goes on and on. Even The Two-Gun Kid became one of their reservists for a time.

         By 1985, the group had become so large (even with most members inactive at any given time), that some of its members were spun off into a second series — West Coast Avengers. Hawkeye set up a branch headquarters in Los Angeles, and several of the ongoing characters followed him into a new monthly comic, which lasted until 1994. A third group, calling itself Great Lakes Avengers, never quite got off the ground, nor was it ever officially recognized by the parent group.

          Surprisingly, it took until 1999 for The Avengers to appear on TV — or in any non-comics story media, beyond a paperback novelization or two. That was the year an animated Avengers series joined X-Men and Spider-Man on Fox TV's Saturday morning lineup. Results are not yet in, but as popular as those other recent Marvel shows have been, it seems likely Marvel's most all-encompassing group has finally found its place on the air.
