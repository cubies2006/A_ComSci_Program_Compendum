As A Whole

     In the early 60s, a new kind of superhero was created. Unlike most of his contemporaries, he had not had his powers from birth. He was a normal person who had, through an accident, gained incredible powers. This meant that he had a life, complete with all of life's problems: money, relationships, secret identity, even school. That's right. He was to be the first teen-aged superhero, Spiderman.

     However, when Stan Lee pitched the idea for Spiderman to the editors of Marvel, they were less than enthusiastic. They assured Stan that the entire concept simply wouldn't work. The first problem was his name: Spiderman. Nobody likes spiders. Most people would rather squash a spider than to read about one. Then there was his age problem. Teenagers were not superheroes. Superheroes had already learned about life and had worked through all of the difficulties inherent in being a teenager. Then there was the issue of his personal problems. Superheroes didn't have problems, they solved them. People wouldn't want a superhero who was worrying about paying the mortgage or wondering how he was going to explain why he hadn't completed a homework assignment. People wanted a hero with a squeaky-clean image that everybody looked up to. It seemed that Spiderman was doomed to nonexistence.

     Fortunately for the wall-crawler, Marvel was retiring one of its titles, Amazing Fantasy. Since no one at Marvel would really mind what went into the final issue, Stan decided to put the story of Spiderman's creation in. The response was completely unlike anything Marvel had expected. Spiderman became an almost instant success. People looked at Peter Parker/Spiderman as an equal. He, like everyone else on Earth, was a person doing his best to handle everything that life thrust at him. And his powers were a liability to him almost as often as they were an asset. He was a hero who experienced pain, loss, grief and defeat, but never let any of that stop him from doing what he knew was right. While he was saving the city of New York and it's inhabitants from one type of disaster or another, his personal life and the things he most enjoyed doing, had to be sacrificed. People could identify with that aspect of his life and could compare some of the things they had given up for the sake of responsibility.

     Now, it's hard to imagine a world without Spiderman. In addition to the several comic book titles, three different animated series, two live action television series and a hit movie, Spiderman served as an inspiration, a template, or a launching point for literally dozens of other heroes. Every hero who had to wrestle with his own inner struggles, every hero who was constantly striving for the respect of those he was saving, and every hero who sometimes wished that he had not received his powers, owes a debt to Spiderman. He was the one who showed us that a hero, even a superhero, is still just a person that has to face the same things that each one of us face every day. We are all very fortunate that Stan 'The Man' Lee not only had the vision to dream him, but the perseverance to make sure that the character of Spiderman became a reality.
